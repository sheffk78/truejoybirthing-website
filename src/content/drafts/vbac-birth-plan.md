# VBAC Birth Plan: How to Write One That Actually Works

You've had a C-section before. Now you're exploring a vaginal birth after cesarean — a VBAC — and you need a birth plan that reflects *that* specific reality. Not a generic template. Not wishful thinking. A plan that acknowledges what the hospital actually requires, what your provider will push back on, and how to hold your ground where it matters.

I'm Shelbi, a certified birth doula and mom of seven. I've walked alongside VBAC moms in hospitals across Texas, and I want to help you build a plan that's clear, realistic, and truly yours.

Let's walk through it together.

---

## What VBAC Means for Your Birth Plan

A VBAC birth plan isn't just another birth plan with "VBAC" written at the top. The reality is that choosing a trial of labor after cesarean (TOLAC) comes with specific medical considerations, hospital policies, and provider preferences that shape what's possible.

Here's what makes a VBAC plan different:

- **Continuous monitoring is almost always required.** Most hospitals mandate electronic fetal monitoring throughout labor for VBAC patients. This doesn't mean you're trapped in bed, but it does mean your mobility options are more limited than an unmedicated, low-risk birth plan.
- **Your provider's philosophy matters a lot.** Some providers are genuinely supportive of VBAC. Others say they are but default to a repeat C-section at the first sign of a longer labor. Your birth plan needs to reflect conversations you've *already had* with your provider — not just what you hope will happen.
- **Hospital policy can override your preferences.** Not every hospital allows VBACs. Of those that do, some have strict protocols about timing, induction methods, and anesthesia availability. Knowing your hospital's policies before you write your plan saves you from disappointment later.
- **There's a real conversation about risk.** The risk of uterine rupture during a VBAC is approximately 0.5–0.9% for a low-transverse incision, according to ACOG. That's low, but it's not zero, and your plan needs to show you understand that — not so you're afraid, but so your team knows you've done your homework.

Your VBAC birth plan should be a document that any nurse or provider can read in five minutes and know exactly where you stand. That means being specific about your preferences while staying flexible enough to handle the unexpected.

## What to Include in Your VBAC Birth Plan

Think of your birth plan as a conversation starter, not a contract. You're telling your team, "Here's what I prefer, and here's what I want you to know about me." The sections below cover what to address.

### Trial of Labor Preferences

This is the heart of your VBAC plan. Be direct about what you want.

- **State clearly that you are attempting a VBAC.** Don't leave this ambiguous. Write something like: "I am planning a trial of labor after cesarean and intend to deliver vaginally if it is safe to do so."
- **Address your previous C-section.** Briefly note the reason for your prior cesarean (e.g., "First C-section was for breech presentation — not a failure to progress"). This helps your team understand that your VBAC may have favorable odds.
- **Set expectations for labor progression.** VBAC labors are not managed the same way as first-time labors. State your preference for allowing labor to progress without arbitrary time limits, while acknowledging that your provider will make the final call on safety.
- **If you've also planned for a possible C-section:** Link to your [C-section birth plan](/c-section-birth-plan/) for those contingencies, and note in your VBAC plan that you have one ready.

### Monitoring

Most hospitals require continuous electronic fetal monitoring during a VBAC. Fighting this in your birth plan isn't realistic and can create tension with your team from the start.

Instead, work *with* the requirement:

- **Request wireless or telemetry monitoring** if your hospital has it, which allows you to move around more freely.
- **Ask for intermittent monitoring during early labor** if your provider is open to it — some will allow it, some won't.
- **Note your preference for being informed** about what the monitor is showing. You deserve to know what your team is seeing, not just be hooked up to a machine with no explanation.

### Pain Management

Your pain management preferences are personal, and VBAC doesn't automatically mean you should or shouldn't choose an epidural. Here's what to consider:

- **If you want an epidural:** State that clearly. Some providers recommend early epidural placement for VBAC patients in case an emergency C-section becomes necessary. This isn't a scare tactic — it's logistics, and knowing it ahead of time helps you plan.
- **If you prefer unmedicated labor:** Say so, and also state that you're open to pain relief if labor becomes prolonged or if a C-section is needed. Being rigid here can work against you.
- **If you're planning an induction** as part of your VBAC, the pain management conversation changes — the [birth plan for induction](/birth-plan-for-induction/) walks through those specifics in more detail.
- **Non-pharmacological comfort measures** you'd like to use: movement, position changes, hydrotherapy (if available), doula support, breathing techniques. List these even if you're planning an epidural — they're helpful in early labor regardless.

### Delivery Preferences

This section covers what happens as you approach pushing and delivery:

- **Positions for pushing.** Lateral, hands-and-knees, squatting — whatever works for your body and your monitoring setup.
- **Who you want in the room.** Beyond your partner, note if you'd like your doula present.
- **Skin-to-skin preferences.** State whether you want immediate skin-to-skin if baby is stable.
- **Delayed cord clamping.** If this is important to you, say so.
- **Who announces the sex.** If you're finding out at birth, note who should announce it.
- **Photography or video preferences.** Some hospitals have policies; knowing yours helps.

### Recovery Preferences

Planning for recovery after a vaginal delivery is different than after a C-section, but you should be prepared for both:

- **If you deliver vaginally:** Note your preferences for postpartum monitoring, breastfeeding support, and when you'd like to go home (if early discharge is an option).
- **If you end up with a repeat C-section:** Note that you have a separate [C-section birth plan](/c-section-birth-plan/) and that your recovery preferences are detailed there.
- **In either case:** State whether you want your doula to remain with you in recovery, and any specific postpartum support you'd like from the nursing staff.

### If Things Change

This is the section that shows your team you're not naive. VBAC doesn't always go according to plan, and acknowledging that builds trust.

- **State clearly:** "I understand that a repeat cesarean may become necessary for my safety or my baby's safety. If that happens, I would like to be informed and involved in the decision-making process as much as possible."
- **In the event of a C-section:** Note your preferences for the operating room — partner present, conscious delivery, skin-to-skin in the OR if possible.
- **In the event of an emergency:** State that you trust your medical team to act quickly and that you've already discussed your preferences with your partner, who can advocate for you if you're unable to.

## What to Skip in Your VBAC Birth Plan

Less is more. A birth plan that reads like a novel gets skimmed, not respected. Skip these:

- **Lengthy explanations of your birth philosophy.** Your team doesn't need a page on why you believe in natural birth. They need to know your preferences.
- **Demands that conflict with hospital policy.** If your hospital requires continuous monitoring for VBAC, don't write "I decline continuous monitoring." That creates an adversarial dynamic before labor even starts.
- **Overly specific timeframes.** "I want to push for no more than 30 minutes" — this ties your provider's hands and ignores the reality that every labor is different.
- **Avalanche of preferences.** Three pages of bullet points will lose your reader. Aim for one page that covers what matters most.
- **Anything passive-aggressive.** "I do not want to be pressured into a C-section" is adversarial. Try: "I would like to be informed and included in any conversation about changing my birth plan."

## Questions to Ask at Your VBAC Appointment

Before you write your birth plan, have a real conversation with your provider. Bring these questions:

1. **Am I a good candidate for a VBAC?** Ask specifically about your incision type, the reason for your previous C-section, and any factors that might affect your chances.
2. **What is your VBAC success rate?** ACOG reports that VBAC success rates are approximately 60–80% overall, but your individual chances depend on your specific history. Your provider should give you a personalized assessment.
3. **What are this hospital's VBAC policies?** Ask about mandatory monitoring, time limits on labor, induction protocols, and anesthesia availability requirements.
4. **Under what circumstances would you recommend a repeat C-section?** You need to know their threshold — is it at the first sign of a slow labor, or will they support you through a longer process?
5. **How do you handle induction for VBAC patients?** Some providers won't induce at all. Others will use certain methods but not others. Know this going in.
6. **What happens if I go into labor before my scheduled C-section?** If you end up needing a scheduled repeat C-section (backup plan), ask whether they'd still support a VBAC if labor starts on its own first.
7. **Who will be on call when I deliver?** If your provider isn't available, who covers? Are they equally VBAC-supportive?
8. **Will a doctor be in the hospital throughout my labor?** ACOG recommends that a physician capable of performing a C-section be "immediately available" during a trial of labor. Ask what that looks like at your hospital.

## VBAC-Friendly Hospitals in Texas

Hospital policy can make or break your VBAC experience. Not every hospital allows them, and of those that do, support varies widely. Here's what to know in six major Texas metro areas:

### Dallas–Fort Worth

Several DFW hospitals are known for supporting VBAC, but policies vary. Ask specifically about telemetry monitoring, whether anesthesia is required to be in-house during your labor, and what their timeline looks like for recommending a repeat C-section during a prolonged labor. Medical City and some Baylor Scott & White locations have historically been more VBAC-supportive, but **always verify current policy directly** — these change.

### Houston

Houston has large hospital systems with a range of VBAC policies. Texas Children's Hospital and Memorial Hermann have protocols in place for VBAC, but individual provider support still varies. Ask whether your specific OB attends VBACs and what the hospital requires for on-call anesthesia coverage. Houston also has a strong birth center community, though most birth centers cannot attend VBACs — verify before assuming this is an option.

### San Antonio

VBAC access in San Antonio is available at several major hospitals, but the key factor is your provider. Military families should check with their specific MTF about current VBAC policies, as these can differ from civilian hospitals. Methodist and University Health have supported VBAC in recent years, but again, **confirm directly** before committing to a provider or hospital.

### Austin

Austin has a vibrant birth community and several hospitals that support VBAC. St. David's and Seton systems have historically offered VBAC with appropriate protocols. Austin also tends to have more providers who are openly supportive of physiologic birth, which can make your VBAC experience smoother. Still — ask the hard questions at your appointment.

### Fort Worth

Fort Worth hospitals have become more VBAC-friendly in recent years, but access still depends heavily on the individual provider. Some practices have a blanket "no VBAC" policy; others will support you on a case-by-case basis. If your current provider doesn't attend VBACs, it's better to find out early and switch than to fight a battle in the delivery room.

### El Paso

VBAC options in El Paso are available but more limited than in the larger metros. University Medical Center and some private hospitals have protocols in place. If you're birthing at a military facility, check current VBAC policies. For any El Paso hospital, confirm that anesthesia coverage meets ACOG's "immediately available" recommendation before you commit.

> **Important:** Hospital policies change, and individual providers within a hospital system can have very different approaches to VBAC. Always call the hospital's labor and delivery unit directly and ask about their current VBAC policy. Don't rely solely on what you read online — even here.

## How a Doula Helps During a VBAC

I'm biased, obviously. But here's the truth: VBAC moms benefit enormously from doula support, and the research backs this up.

A doula during a VBAC provides:

- **Emotional support through the unknown.** You've birthed before, but not this way. A doula holds space for the hope and the fear simultaneously — without projecting either onto you.
- **Advocacy when you're in the thick of it.** During labor is not the time to negotiate with your provider about whether you can labor another hour. Your doula can help you ask the right questions and make sure your voice is heard.
- **Physical comfort techniques that work WITH monitoring.** We know you're probably going to be on a monitor. Your doula should know how to work with that — helping you move within the constraints, use position changes, and manage labor without fighting the technology.
- **Help processing your previous birth.** If your first birth was traumatic or disappointing, that doesn't just disappear because you're trying for a VBAC. A good doula will help you work through that *before* labor starts so it doesn't hijack your experience this time.
- **Support no matter how birth unfolds.** If your VBAC is successful, your doula celebrates with you. If you end up with a repeat C-section, your doula stays — helping you understand what happened, holding your hand in the OR, and making sure you still feel respected and informed.

## VBAC Birth Plan FAQ

### What's the VBAC success rate?

ACOG reports that approximately 60–80% of people who attempt a VBAC deliver vaginally. Your individual success rate depends on factors like the reason for your previous C-section, the type of incision you had, how far along you are in pregnancy, and whether labor starts on its own. If your first C-section was for a non-recurring reason (like breech presentation), your chances are generally on the higher end of that range.

### Is VBAC safer than a repeat C-section?

It's not a simple yes or no — it depends on what you're measuring. A successful vaginal birth has fewer complications than a C-section (shorter recovery, lower infection risk, less blood loss). A VBAC attempt that ends in a repeat C-section does carry slightly more risk than a planned repeat C-section. The overall risk of serious complications with either option is low. ACOG considers both VBAC and repeat C-section to be reasonable choices for most people with one prior low-transverse cesarean. Talk through your specific risk factors with your provider.

### Can I be induced during a VBAC?

It depends. Some providers will induce a VBAC; others won't. ACOG says that induction of labor may be considered in certain circumstances for VBAC candidates, but certain medications (like Misoprostol/Cytotec) are generally not recommended for VBAC induction due to increased rupture risk. This is a conversation you absolutely need to have with your provider well before your due date.

### What are the signs of uterine rupture?

This is important to know, but I don't want you to fixate on it — the risk is very low. Signs your team will be watching for include:

- **Abnormal fetal heart rate patterns** (this is the most common and most reliable sign — which is why continuous monitoring is required)
- **Loss of station** (the baby moves back up the birth canal)
- **Sudden onset of severe abdominal pain** that's different from normal labor
- **Vaginal bleeding**
- **Decreased uterine tone or a change in the shape of your abdomen**

Your medical team monitors for these signs continuously. You don't need to diagnose uterine rupture yourself — that's their job — but knowing the signs means you can speak up if something feels wrong.

### Can I have a VBAC after two C-sections?

It's possible, but it's a different conversation. ACOG notes that VBAC after two prior low-transverse cesareans "may be considered" but that the evidence is more limited. The risk of uterine rupture is slightly higher than with one prior C-section. You'll need a provider who is specifically experienced with VBA2C, and not all hospitals will allow it. Be honest with yourself and your provider about the risks, and make sure your hospital has the resources to respond quickly if needed.

### What if I go into labor before my scheduled C-section?

If you've scheduled a repeat C-section as a backup plan but start labor on your own, talk to your provider about whether a VBAC attempt is still an option. Many providers will support a trial of labor if you're already in spontaneous labor and both you and baby are doing well. This is a great conversation to have *before* you're actually in labor.

### Does my hospital allow VBACs?

Not all hospitals do. ACOG recommends that hospitals offering VBAC have the ability to perform an emergency C-section "immediately available" — meaning anesthesia and surgical staff are on-site. Some smaller or rural hospitals don't have this capacity and therefore don't allow VBACs. **Call your hospital's labor and delivery unit directly and ask about their current VBAC policy.** Don't assume — policies change, and what was true two years ago may not be true today.
